Open a Fidelity Account as a Non-Resident Alien

April 26, 2017
Life invest , tax

Inspired by my friends, I realized that it is not a good choice to put all assets in a checking account without interests, because of the inflation. A saving account may be the simplest solution to currency devaluation. However, most U.S. online banks require a client to be a U.S. person. Especially, Discover and Ally only accept U.S. citizens and permanent residents. Thus I gave up saving accounts and started searching for alternatives.

Finally I decided to open a Fidelity account to invest in mutual funds. At the last step of online application, again I encountered the immigrant status problem:

I certify that I am a U.S. citizen or other U.S. person as defined in the IRS Form W-9 instructions.

According to the definition by IRS (Internal Revenue Service), most international students, like me, are considered as non resident aliens for tax purpose during the first 5 calendar years. U.S. persons include U.S. citizens, permanent residents, and resident aliens, but exclude non resident aliens (see the definition in Form-W9). It seemed that I must stop here.

But later I found Fidelity allows clients to file Form W-8. As far as I knew, Form W-8BEN is used to tell a bank that you are not a U.S. person. Then I gave a call to Fidelity and the representative told me that I could first check that U.S. person checkbox and open an account, and later file W-8BEN to change my profile. Great! I quickly finished online application and got an individual account. After that I opened that page, planning to submit W-8BEN, only to be told that no action was required according to their records. I thought Fidelity might need some time to review my application and determine those additional required materials. So I decided to wait for some days.

My guess was right. The next morning Fidelity reminded me to file W-8. After carefully filling out W-8 and clicking “submit”, I thought everything was done.

Then that evening I received the second message that my account had been closed. Since it was Saturday evening and hot lines were not available, I immediately chatted with a representative online. She explained that they closed my account temporarily since it was the first time that I opened an account with them, and told me to wait until Monday when they finished reviewing my account.

Monday afternoon I called Fidelity again. This time the representative insisted on that I must file Form W-9, just as the date point from US Credit Card Guide. I talked with that representative for ten minutes and tried using the official guide on the website of IRS to defend that I should submit W-8BEN instead of W-9.

A nonresident alien individual should not deliver Form W-9 to a U.S. bank, U.S. savings and loan association, U.S. credit union, or U.S. insurance company. Instead, he should deliver Form W-8BEN, Certificate of Foreign Status of Beneficial Owner for United States Tax Withholding, to such institutions ……

But it did not work. She was very firm. Finally I gave up and filed W-9 as she required. Maybe I was wrong since Fidelity is a brokerage service and does not belong to any one of the categories listed above. Anyway after that all constraints on my account were released and “everything is good now”.

However, after one week, I received a paper mail from Fidelity, which wrote they could not accept my Form W8-BEN since I provided a U.S. address and a U.S. phone number. Literally, how can a non resident alien resides in America? they required me to explain this. An explanation model was also included in that mail. Fine! I checked that “I am an F1 student so I temporarily present in U.S.” and wrote “I need a phone number to contact my professor”.

It seemed that everything was really done after I sent out my reply. In the following two weeks I did not receive any further messages.

This is how I finally opened a Fidelity account as a non resident alien.
